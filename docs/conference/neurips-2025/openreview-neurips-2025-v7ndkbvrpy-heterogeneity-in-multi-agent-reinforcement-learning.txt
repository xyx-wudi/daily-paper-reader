## Abstract 

Heterogeneity is a fundamental property in multi-agent reinforcement learning 1

(MARL), which is closely related not only to the functional differences of agents, 2

but also to policy diversity and environmental interactions. However, the MARL 3

field currently lacks a rigorous definition and deeper understanding of heterogeneity. 4

This paper systematically discusses heterogeneity in MARL from the perspectives 5

of definition , quantification , and utilization . First, based on an agent-level modeling 6

of MARL, we categorize heterogeneity into five types and provide mathematical 7

definitions. Second, we define the concept of heterogeneity distance and propose 8

a practical quantification method. Third, we design a heterogeneity-based multi-9

agent dynamic parameter sharing algorithm as an example of the application of our 10 

methodology. Case studies demonstrate that our method can effectively identify 11 

and quantify various types of agent heterogeneity. Experimental results show that 12 

the proposed algorithm, compared to other parameter sharing baselines, has better 13 

interpretability and stronger adaptability. The proposed methodology will help the 14 

MARL community gain a more comprehensive and profound understanding of 15 

heterogeneity, and further promote the development of practical algorithms. 16 

## 1 Introduction 17 

Multi-agent reinforcement learning (MARL) has achieved success in various real-world applications, 18 

such as swarm robotic control [Kalashnikov et al., 2018], autonomous driving [Zhou et al., 2021], 19 

and large language model fine-tuning [Ma et al., 2024]. However, most MARL studies focus on 20 

policy learning for homogeneous multi-agent systems (MAS), overlooking in-depth discussions 21 

of heterogeneous multi-agent scenarios [Ning and Xie, 2024]. Heterogeneity is a common phe-22 

nomenon in multi-agent systems. For example, in nature, different species of fish collaborate to 23 

find food [Burns et al., 2019]; in human society, diverse teams demonstrate higher intelligence 24 

and resilience [Dall’Anese et al., 2013, Young, 1993]; and in artificial systems, aerial drones and 25 

ground vehicles cooperate to monitor forest fires [Lwowski et al., 2017]. Heterogeneity can enhance 26 

system functionality, reduce costs, and improve robustness, but effectively leveraging heterogeneity 27 

remains a key challenge in multi-agent system [Bennett, 2024]. As an approach of learning through 28 

environmental interactions, MARL can effectively enable multi-agent systems to learn collabora-29 

tive policies. Hence, exploring heterogeneity from a reinforcement learning perspective would 30 

significantly broaden the applicability of MARL. 31 

In the current MARL field, although some works explicitly or implicitly mention agent hetero-32 

geneity, only a few focus on its definition and identification. Regarding explicit discussion of 33 

heterogeneity, studies have explored communication issues [Seraj et al., 2021], credit assign-34 

ment [Yu et al., 2024], and zero-shot generalization [Guo et al., 2024] in heterogeneous MARL. 35 

However, these works limit their focus to agents with clear functional differences and lack def-36 

initions of agent heterogeneity. On the other hand, many studies explore policy diversity in 37 

MARL. Some encourage agents to learn distinguishable behaviors based on identity or trajec-38

## 3 Definition and Taxonomy of Heterogeneity in MARL 107 

Heterogeneity in MAS. Our goal is to define agent heterogeneity from the perspective of MARL. 108 

Before achieving this, we need to discuss heterogeneity in MAS across various disciplines. Early 109 

studies [Dudek et al., 1996, Parker, 2000] define heterogeneity as differences in physical structure 110 

or functionality of agents, which aligns with common understanding. Later work [Panait and 111 

Luke, 2005] describes heterogeneity as differences in agent behavior, further expanding its meaning. 112 

Recently, [Bennett, 2024] points out that heterogeneity may be a complex phenomenon, related 113 

not only to the inherent properties of agents, but also to their interactions with environment. Thus, 114 

heterogeneity in MARL should not be limited to inherent functional differences of agents, but should 115 

also fully consider various coupling effects of agents within the environment. 116 

Heterogeneity in MARL. In the context of MARL, the fundamental modeling of MARL and its 117 

primal problem provides considerable convenience for defining heterogeneity. This modeling clearly 118 

specifies all MARL elements, delineating the boundaries of the problem discussion 2 and ensuring 119 

the completeness of the discussion. 120 

We focus on the heterogeneity among agents within a same POMG. As discussed in Section 2, the 121 

function in a POMG can connect agent-level elements. Therefore, we categorize agent heterogeneity 122 

into five types centered around the functions. This approach not only avoids overly redundant 123 

classification but also ensures comprehensive coverage of each agent-level element. Regarding 124 

definition, the condition for heterogeneity is obtained by taking the negation of the necessary and 125 

sufficient conditions for homogeneity. 126 

Specifically, these five types of heterogeneity and their related definitions are as follows: 127 

• Observation heterogeneity describes the differences of agents in observing global information. The 128 

relevant elements include the agent’s observation space and observation function. 129 

Definition 1. Agents i and j are observation heterogeneous if the following conditions do not hold at 130 

the same time: ➀ Oi = Oj ; ➁ ∀ˆs ∈ { Si}i∈N , Ωi(·| ˆs) = Ω j (·| ˆs).131

• Effect transition heterogeneity describes the differences of agents in how their states and actions 137

In the five types of heterogeneity mentioned above, we assume that all functions follow the Markov 154 

property, making them independent of the agent’s trajectory. Therefore, the first four types of 155 

heterogeneity can be considered environment-related, which reflect the heterogeneity in the MARL 156 

primal problem. The last type describes the policy heterogeneity of agents before, during, and after 157 

training, which reflects the heterogeneity of optimization objectives (policies) in the primal problem. 158

Practical Method. Although the heterogeneity distance has a simple form, some issues may arise dur-179 

ing practical computation. First, computing the distribution distance via sampling is computationally 180 

complex, while computing the distance using analytical solutions requires knowing the distribution 181 

type. In real-world scenarios, the distributions may be unknown or of different types 4. Second, the 182 

independent variable space may be very large, making traversal-based computation infeasible. 183 

For the first issue, our approach is to standardize the original distributions . By learning a rep-184 

resentation mapping, for all independent variables x, a measurable distribution pi(z|x) is used to 185 

capture the characteristics of the original distribution Fi(y|x), replacing the original one for measure 186 

computation. For the second issue, our approach is sampling based on the interaction between 187 

agents and the environment . Instead of simply traversing the space or using random policy explo-188 

ration for sampling, we construct a sample pool using trajectories from the training phase of MARL. 189 

This significantly reduces computational load and filters out excessive marginal spaces that interfere 190 

with MARL, benefiting the use of heterogeneity distance in subsequent MARL tasks (Section 5). 191 

Combining these ideas, we propose a practical method as shown in Figure 2. 192 

In the first step , the agents interact with the environment during MARL training to build a sample 193 

pool. Notably, the sample pool data is shuffled to ensure that the learned function follows the Markov 194 

property (independent of historical information), similar to the original function. 195 

In the second step , the representational distributions are learned. We discuss this in both model-196 

based and model-free settings, corresponding to cases where the function is known and unknown, 197 

respectively. We adopt the conditional variational autoencoder (CVAE) framework [Sohn et al., 2015] 198 

for representation learning. In the model-based case, CVAE performs a reconstruction task [Lopez-199 

Martin et al., 2017]. The optimization goal is to maximize the likelihood of the reconstructed variable 200 

log p(y|x). Through derivation, we obtain the evidence lower bound (ELBO) as: 201 

ELBO model-based = Efϕ(z|y,x ) [log gω (y|z, x )] − DKL [fϕ(z|y, x ) ∥ p(z|x)] , (4) where fϕ and gω represent the encoder and decoder, respectively, and p(z|x) is the prior conditional 202 

latent distribution. We designed the relevant loss based on ELBO, including a reconstruction term 203 

and a prior-matching term. The derivation for this part can be found in Appendix H. 204

## Quantification: An Application 254 

Based on the case study in Section 4.2, the pro-255 

posed method can not only accurately quantify all 256 

types of heterogeneity, but also the comprehen-257 

sive heterogeneity among agents. Additionally, the method is independent of the parameter-sharing 258 

type used in MARL and can be deployed online, thereby further enhancing its practicality. In 259 

this section, we provide a practical application of our methodology to demonstrate its potential in 260 

empowering MARL. 261 

We select parameter sharing in MARL as our application context. As a common technique in 262 

MARL, parameter sharing can reduce computational consumption while improving sample utilization 263 

efficiency [KIM and Sung, 2023], but its excessive use may inhibit agents’ policy heterogeneity 264 

expression [Hu et al., 2024]. Many works have attempted to find a balance between parameter sharing 265 

and policy heterogeneity [Li et al., 2024b]. However, existing approaches suffer from two main 266 

problems: poor interpretability , unable to explain why policy heterogeneity is necessary and to what 267 

extent; and poor adaptability , manifested by numerous task-specific hyperparameters and inability 268 

to dynamically adapt policy training. (For a more detailed discussion of these algorithms, see the 269 

experimental section 6.1) 270 

To address these issues, we propose a Heterogeneity-based multi-agent Dynamic Parameter Sharing 271 

algorithm (HetDPS) with two core ideas(More details can be found in Appendix I): 272 

♠ Grouping agents for parameter sharing through heterogeneity distances . We utilize distance-273 

based clustering methods to group agents, thus avoiding the introduction of task-specific hyperpa-274 

rameters like group number [Christianos et al., 2021, Li et al., 2024a] or fusion thresholds [Hu et al., 275 

2024]. The heterogeneity distance matrices also enhance the algorithm’s interpretability. 276 

♣ Periodically quantifying heterogeneity and modifying agents’ parameter sharing paradigm .277 

This can help the sample pool become more aligned with policy training. This approach can also 278 

help policies escape local optima [Lyle et al., 2024], the effectiveness of such a mechanism has been 279 

7verified in the MARL domain [Li et al., 2024b], and even in broader RL areas such as large model 280 

fine-tuning [Noukhovitch et al., 2023, Ma et al., 2024]. 281

12 A Limitations 482 

Although our proposed heterogeneity distance can effectively quantify agent heterogeneity and 483 

identify various potential heterogeneities, there remain some limitations in its practical implementa-484 

tion. One limitation is in scaling with the number of agents. Typically, the heterogeneity distance 485 

quantification algorithm outputs a heterogeneity distance matrix for the entire multi-agent system, 486 

with a computational complexity of O(N 2). When the number of agents increases significantly, 487 

matrix computation becomes costly. However, if only studying heterogeneity between specific agents 488 

in the MAS is required, the method remains effective. One only needs to remove data from other 489 

agents during CVAE training and sampling computation. 490 

Additionally, the practical algorithms for heterogeneity quantification are built on the assumption 491 

that agent-related variables are vectors. If certain agent variables, such as observation inputs, are 492 

multimodal, operations like padding in the proposed algorithm become difficult to implement. But 493 

this does not affect the correctness of the theory. As the relevant theory still holds in this situation, 494 

additional tricks are needed for practical calculation implementation. 495 

## B Broader Impacts 496 

Our work systematically analyzes heterogeneity in MARL, which has strong correlations with a 497 

series of works in MARL. Under our theoretical framework, research on agent policy diversity in 498 

MARL can be categorized within the domain of policy heterogeneity. Our work can give a new 499 

perspective for studying policy diversity. Our proposed quantification methods can not only help 500 

these works with policy evolution analysis but also explain the relationship between policy diversity 501 

and agent heterogeneity. Furthermore, our proposed HetDPS, as an application case, can also be 502 

classified among parameter sharing-based works. 503 

Additionally, some traditional heterogeneous MARL works can be categorized within environment-504 

related heterogeneity domains. Our quantification and definition methods are orthogonal to these 505 

works, which can fully utilize our proposed methodology for further advancement. For instance, 506 

observation heterogeneity quantification can be used to enhance agents’ ability to aggregate hetero-507 

geneous observation information; transition heterogeneity quantification can help design intrinsic 508 

rewards to assist heterogeneous multi-agents in learning cooperative policies. 509 

In conclusion, our work not only expands the scope of heterogeneity in MARL but also closely 510 

connects with many current hot topics, contributing to the further development of these works. 511 

## C An introduction to POMG 512 

Partially Observable Markov Game (POMG) is essentially an extension of Partially Observable 513 

Markov Decision Process (POMDP), which in turn extends Markov Decision Process (MDP). 514 

MDP [Bellman, 1957, Kaelbling et al., 1996] is a mathematical framework that describes sequential 515 

decision-making by a single agent in a fully observable environment. In an MDP, the agent can 516 

fully observe the environment’s state, select actions based on the current state, and aim to maximize 517 

cumulative rewards. Compared to MDP, the key extension of POMDP [Kaelbling et al., 1998, 518 

Cassandra, 1998] is the consideration of partial observability, making it suitable for modeling both 519 

single-agent partially observable problems [Spaan, 2012] and multi-agent problems [Bernstein et al., 520 

2002, Oliehoek et al., 2016]. In multi-agent POMDPs, agents typically operate in a fully cooperative 521 

mode, where their rewards are usually team-shared. 522 

The key extension of POMG over POMDP lies in modeling mixed game relationships among multiple 523 

agents. Unlike POMDP, agents in POMG do not share a common reward function; instead, each 524 

agent has its own (agent-level) reward function, making POMG more general [Sun et al., 2023, 525 

Gronauer and Diepold, 2022]. This design enables POMG to handle competitive, cooperative, and 526 

mixed interaction scenarios, better reflecting the complexity of real-world multi-agent systems. The 527 

logical relationships among Markov decision processes and their variants are illustrated in Figure 7 528 

and Figure 8. As shown in these figures, POMG is the most general framework for modeling original 529 

problems in the MARL domain. For these reasons, we chose POMG as the foundation for discussing 530 

heterogeneity in MARL. 531

## D Other potential types of heterogeneity in MARL 532 

Benefiting from the reinforcement learning modeling based on POMG, we have clearly defined the 533 

boundaries of heterogeneity discussed in this paper. In fact, within the realm of unconventional 534 

multi-agent systems, there might be other types of heterogeneity. 535 

For instance, agents may have different length of decision timesteps, with some agents inclined 536 

towards long-term high-level decisions, while others tend to make short-term low-level decisions. 537 

Agents may also have different discount factors, some works try to assign varying discount factors to 538 

different agents during algorithm training [Nguyen et al., 2022], to encourage agents to develop " my-539 

opic " or " far-sighted " policy behaviors, thereby promoting agent cooperation. However, differences 540 

in discount factors are more reflective of algorithmic design variations rather than environmental 541 

distinctions, and thus fall outside the scope of this paper. Moreover, there may be heterogeneity 542 

among agents regarding communication, agents might have different communication channels due 543 

to hardware variations. However, the establishment of communication protocols aims to enable 544 

agents to receive more information when making decisions, potentially overcoming non-stationarity 545 

and partial observability issues [Gronauer and Diepold, 2022]. These communication messages are 546 

essentially mappings of global information processed in the environment, which are then input into 547 

the action-related network modules. From this perspective, agent communication can be modeled as 548 

a more generalized observation function that maps global information to local observations for agent 549 

decision-making, and communication heterogeneity can be categorized under observation hetero-550 

geneity. From a learning perspective, agents might also have heterogeneous available knowledge, 551 

such as differences in initial basic policies or variations in supplementary knowledge accessible 552 

during execution phase. Moreover, heterogeneity might extend beyond abstract issues, including 553 

computational resource differences among agents during learning. 554 

Overall, even from the perspective of multi-agent reinforcement learning, heterogeneity in multi-agent 555 

systems remains a domain with extensive discussion space, warranting further subsequent research. 556

## G Meta-Transition and its Heterogeneity Distance 645 

To quantify an agent’s comprehensive heterogeneity, we introduce the concept of meta-transition. 646 

Meta-transition is a modeling approach that explores an agent’s own attributes from its perspec-647 

tive. Our goal is to quantify an agent’s comprehensive heterogeneity using only the agent’s local 648 

information (as global information is typically difficult to obtain in practical MARL scenarios). 649 

Based on this, we provide the definition of meta-transition. Let the meta-transition of agent i be 650 

denoted as Mi. It is a mapping Mi : Si × Ai → Si × R × Ωi. At time step t, the inputs of 651 

meta-transition are the agent’s local state sit and local action ait, and the outputs are the next time 652 

step’s local state sit+1 , the next time step’s local observation oit+1 , and the current time step’s reward 653 

rit based on the state and action. 654 

We explain why the above relationship can reflect all agent-level elements in POMG. The input local 655 

state and local action of meta-transition actually correspond to the inverse mapping to the global 656 

state and global action. This inverse mapping potentially restores the local state and action to global 657 

information, and then obtains the next time step’s global state according to the global state transition 658 

function, which is mapped to local observation through the observation function. Therefore, this 659 

process reflects the agent’s effect transition heterogeneity and observation heterogeneity. Additionally, 660 

the potential global state and global action also determine the agent’s local state and corresponding 661 

reward at the next time step, which reflect the agent’s response transition heterogeneity and objective 662 

heterogeneity, respectively. 663 

17 It is worth noting that meta-transition is not a function that actually exists in POMG, but an implicitly 664 

defined mapping. We aim to quantify this mapping difference to capture the agent’s comprehensive 665 

heterogeneity. Therefore, meta-transition heterogeneity is quantified in a model-free manner. 666 

Moreover, meta-transition is not limited to the aforementioned form. It can be transformed into dif-667 

ferent forms according to the modular settings of independent and dependent variables. For example, 668 

by removing the agent’s reward, meta-transition can reflect the agent’s observation heterogeneity, 669 

response transition heterogeneity, and effect transition heterogeneity. 670 

After determining the input and output of meta-transition, the relevant heterogeneity distance can 671 

be calculated using the same model-free method as before. Since meta-transition involves multiple 672 

variables, and the dimensions between these variables may differ significantly (for example, the 673 

dimension of reward is 1, while the dimension of observation might be 100), directly fitting with 674 

deep networks may struggle to capture information corresponding to low-dimensional variables. We 675 

address this issue through a dimension replication trick. In practice, we typically replicate the reward 676 

dimension to be similar to the dimensions of observation or action, ensuring that the autoencoder 677 

network can capture information related to objective heterogeneity during learning. 678

## I Details of HetDPS 698 

HetDPS is a novel algorithm designed to efficiently manage the allocation of neural network parame-699 

ters across multiple agents in MARL. This algorithm leverages the Wasserstein distance matrix to 700 

cluster agents based on their similarities, and subsequently assigns them to suitable neural networks. 701 

The pseudocode of HetDPS is shown in Algorithm 1. 702 

The algorithm begins by computing the affinity matrix from the Wasserstein distance matrix, which 703 

is then used as input to the Affinity Propagation clustering algorithm. This process yields a new 704 

set of cluster assignments for the agents. If it is the first time the algorithm is executed, the cluster 705 

assignments are directly used as network assignments. 706 

In subsequent iterations, the algorithm compares the new cluster assignments with the previous ones 707 

to determine the optimal network assignments. This is achieved by constructing an overlap matrix 708 

that captures the similarity between the old and new cluster assignments. Based on the number of old 709 

and new clusters, the algorithm handles three distinct cases: 710 

1. Equal number of old and new clusters: In this scenario, the algorithm establishes a one-to-one 711 

mapping between the old and new clusters using the Hungarian algorithm. It then constructs a 712 

mapping from old clusters to networks and assigns each agent to a network based on its new cluster 713 

assignment. 714 

2. More new clusters than old clusters: When the number of new clusters exceeds the number of old 715 

clusters, the algorithm handles network splitting. It uses the Hungarian algorithm to find the best 716 

matching between old and new clusters and establishes a mapping from new clusters to old clusters. 717 

For new clusters without a clear match, the algorithm either finds the most similar old cluster or 718 

identifies the closest network. It then executes a splitting operation to copy parameters from the 719 

source network to the new network. 720 

3. More old clusters than new clusters: In this case, the algorithm handles network merging. It uses 721 

the Hungarian algorithm to find the best matching between old and new clusters and establishes a 722 

mapping from old clusters to new clusters. For each new cluster, it identifies the networks to be 723 

merged and executes a merging operation based on the specified merge mode (majority, random, 724 

average, or weighted). The algorithm then assigns each agent to a network based on its new cluster 725 

assignment. 726 

HetDPS offers a flexible and efficient approach to managing neural network parameters in multi-agent 727 

systems. By dynamically adjusting network assignments based on agent similarities, the algorithm 728 

enables effective parameter sharing and reduces the need for redundant computations. 729
