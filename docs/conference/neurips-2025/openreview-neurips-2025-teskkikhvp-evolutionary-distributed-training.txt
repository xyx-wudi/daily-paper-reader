## Abstract 

We introduce Evolutionary Distributed Training (EDT), a nature-inspired approach 1

to distributed model training. EDT replaces centralized gradient synchronization 2

with evaluation, pairwise model crossover, and mutation, enabling communication-3

efficient training across loosely connected devices. While early investigations show 4

limited effectiveness in language model pretraining, EDT demonstrates strong 5

potential in reinforcement learning (RL). In complex multi-agent environments, 6

EDT facilitates diverse reward exploration and emergent strategies by evolving 7

both policy and reward functions, outperforming traditional training in adaptability 8

and strategic diversity. We also hypothesize EDT as a promising framework for 9

post-training and alignment, offering optimization towards multi-objective, non-10 

differentiable goals. This work positions EDT as a scalable, evolutionary recipe 11 

for distributed learning, offering early insights into where it may best fit within the 12 

deep learning landscape. 13 

## 1 Introduction 14 

The last decade of machine learning has been defined by scale. Transformer-based language models 15 

have demonstrated remarkable emergent capabilities when scaled to billions or even trillions of 16 

parameters, prompting discussions of “Sparks of Artificial General Intelligence” [ 2]. Recent success 17 

with Reinforcement Learning via Verifiable Reward (RLVR) brings us a step closer to human-level 18 

general intelligence [ 18 ][ 22 ]. Towards that goal, we ponder a more fundamental question — How 19 

did intelligence come to be in the first place? A simple answer would be — natural evolution. From 20 

the collective mind of an ant colony to the all-powerful human brain, intelligence emerges from an 21 

evolutionary algorithm. In this work, we intend to investigate how evolutionary algorithms may fit 22 

into the current landscape of deep learning. More specifically, we make two observations about nature: 23 

1. Natural evolution is highly parallelized and decentralized. 2. Most complex organisms in nature 24 

perform pairwise reproduction. From this, we propose Evolutionary Distributed Training (EDT), a 25 

nature-inspired recipe for applying evolutionary algorithms as an outer layer to distributed training. 26 

Models are evaluated locally, selected based on fitness, and recombined via pairwise crossover 27 

and mutation to produce offspring. This process repeats iteratively, without requiring centralized 28 

synchronization of gradients. In the following sections we will conduct some early exploration of 29 

EDT on Large Language Model (LLM) Pre-training, Post-training, and Reinforcement Learning 30 

(RL). 31 

## 2 Language Model Pretraining 32 

2.1 Motivation 33 

Recent work on distributed language model training has introduced new ways to scale model training 34 

beyond traditional data-parallelism. In particular, DiLoCo [ 5], a variant of federated averaging [ 11 ]35 

Submitted to 39th Conference on Neural Information Processing Systems (NeurIPS 2025). Do not distribute. presents a promising distributed optimization algorithm that matches the performance of data-parallel 36 

while communicating 500 times less [ 5]. DiLoCo works by periodically synchronizing model deltas 37 

with a central aggregator using SGD with momentum and Nesterov acceleration. Inspired by the 38 

biological process of pairwise reproduction, we propose a decentralized alternative to DiLoCo using 39 

our Evolutionary Distributed Training (EDT) recipe. Rather than averaging deltas across all workers, 40 

we perform pairwise averaging, producing offspring that inherit characteristics from two parent 41 

models. Our hope is that the momentum accumulated over an ancestry might act as a larger batch, 42 

while the evolutionary algorithm optimizes for better-performing ancestries that carry the right 43 

momentum. 44 

2.2 Method and Experimental Setup 45 

We initialize a 6-million parameter language model based on the LLaMA architecture [ 21 ] and GPT-2 46 

tokenizer [15]. The training dataset is derived from TinyStories [6]. 47 

To adapt the DiLoCo algorithm, we replace global averaging with pairwise model crossover. The 48 

training loop proceeds as follows: 49 

1. Fitness Evaluation: Each model is evaluated on a fixed and shared validation set consisting 50 

of 400 examples .51 

2. Selection: We perform rank-based selection , where models with higher fitness scores are 52 

more likely to be selected as parents. 53 

3. Crossover: For each pair of parent models (A, B ):54 

- Compute the base model as a linear interpolation of their pre-update checkpoints. 55 

- Average their training deltas to form a gradient. 56 

- Average the velocity buffers. 57 

- Apply the gradient to the base model using SGD with momentum and Nesterov acceleration. 58 

- Carry over the resulting model to the next generation. 59 

4. Local Training: Each worker performs 400 inner steps using the AdamW optimizer .60 

We compare the following configurations: 61 

1. DiLoCo Baseline: Running on 8 workers with a per-device batch size of 1 .62 

2. EDT with Rank-Based Pairwise Selection: Running on 8 workers with a per-device 63 

batch size of 1 .64 

3. EDT with Random Pairwise Selection: Running on 8 workers with a per-device batch 65 

size of 1 .66 

4. EDT with Increased Update Frequency: Using 4 × more frequent updates (100 inner 67 

steps) on 8 workers with a per-device batch size of 1 .68 

2.3 Results 69 

Figure 1 presents the validation perplexity across 32 outer steps. We observe that DiLoCo outperforms 70 

EDT significantly over the course of training. While DiLoCo baseline shows signs of continuing 71 

to decline in perplexity, our pairwise method appears to be slowing down to a stop. Furthermore, 72 

rank-based and random pairwise selection yield nearly identical learning curves, indicating that the 73 

evolutionary selection mechanism does not significantly influence model learning in this setting. 74 

Increasing the update frequency in EDT (4x the updates) leads to faster initial perplexity reduction. 75 

However, all configurations converge to similar perplexity values after equivalent total training 76 

steps. This suggests that more frequent mixing may improve short-term convergence but does not 77 

overcome EDT’s inherent limitations in aggregating knowledge or carrying gradient information 78 

across generations. 79 

Given initial exploration results, we conclude that EDT does not seem to fit directly in pre-training 80 

settings. 81

## 3 Reinforcement Learning 82 

3.1 Motivation 83 

Reinforcement Learning (RL) has achieved remarkable success across domains, but remains funda-84 

mentally constrained by its reliance on well-shaped reward functions. Sparse or deceptive reward 85 

landscapes often result in agents that fail to learn meaningful behaviors. If you use a simple reward 86 

function for a complex or long-horizon problem, the model likely will have a hard time learning 87 

anything meaningful due to having too much space to explore. While if you manually create a 88 

complex reward function, you risk the model overfitting and gaming the reward. In the end we are 89 

stuck in an exploration-exploitation dilemma. One solution to this is by scaling up batch size with 90 

massively parallel environments [ 1 ]. Such a method requires a substantial number of rollout workers 91 

[13], which poses significant infrastructural challenges. 92 

3We propose that Evolutionary Distributed Training (EDT) offers a natural mechanism to encourage 93 

exploration without requiring handcrafted rewards or dense feedback. 94 

To this end, we extend EDT to reinforcement learning by treating combinations of reward functions 95 

as mutable DNA sequences . Each worker trains a local policy model using its own reward configu-96 

ration, allowing a population of agents to explore diverse behaviors. Models are selected based on 97 

performance (e.g., Elo rating in self-play), and offspring inherit both policy parameters and modified 98 

reward functions through crossover and mutation. This introduces structured randomness into the 99 

optimization landscape, promoting a balance between exploitation of high-performing strategies and 100 

exploration of novel ones. 101 

Unlike the LLM pretraining setting, where averaging gradients is key, RL is inherently noisy and 102 

local. The decentralized and diverse nature of EDT aligns naturally with RL’s challenges. 103 

Furthermore, consider the following analogy : Say you want to find a treasure. You decided to make 104 

a thousand clones of yourself to do the searching. However, since these are your clones, they behave 105 

similarly, they all have the same habits, same ways of thinking. Maybe you will be able to find the 106 

treasure one day, but in the process you are doing a lot of repeated exploration, looking under the 107 

same rock multiple times. Your search space is confined by your function space [ 12 ]. What if instead 108 

of a thousand you, we have a thousand different people, each with their own quirks and behaviors, 109 

we expand the search space – EDT could promote a more efficient random search for solutions. 110 

3.2 Method and Experimental Setup 111 

We experiment with EDT-RL in a Smash Bros style platform fighting game gym environment [ 10 ]. 112 

Agents control characters that can move, jump, and perform light or heavy attacks in 8 directions, etc, 113 

with the objective of knocking their opponent off the platform. 114 

Model: Each agent is a 64k-parameter transformer-based policy model, trained via Proximal Policy 115 

Optimization (PPO) [17]. 116 

Fitness: We use an Elo rating system based on double-elimination tournaments to evaluate model 117 

performance via self-play matches within the population. 118 

Reward DNA: There are 18 different reward functions, including noop. Each agent is assigned a 119 

“DNA sequence” of size 6 encoding the combination of reward functions. 120 

Training Procedure: 121 

1. Each worker trains a local policy guided by its reward DNA for one generation. 122 

2. After evaluation, agents are selected based on Elo score (fitness). 123 

3. Pairs of parents are chosen to produce offspring: 124 

• Policy crossover : Spherical linear Interpolate weights of parent models. 125 

• DNA crossover : Uniformly mix reward shaping terms from both parents. 126 

4. A mutation step applies to a subset of the offspring, randomly altering components of the 127 

DNA (e.g., replacing and introducing new reward functions). 128 

We compare EDT-RL to a standard Population-Based Training (PBT) setup with fixed, manually 129 

defined reward shapings and identical PPO hyperparameters. 130 

3.3 Results 131 

In the 8-worker setting, EDT-RL produces competitive performance relative to PBT. 132 

Scaling to 32 workers over 40 generations reveals striking emergent behavior. Certain reward 133 

functions, while harmful in isolation, led to novel strategies when combined and refined across 134 

generations. One such mutation was the Floor is Lava penalty, which forces the model to keep 135 

jumping. Another penalized the use of light attacks. While suboptimal at first, these mutations 136 

encouraged exploration of aerial tactics. By generation 20, a new strategy emerged— “ground 137 

slamming” — where an agent jumped above their opponent and executed heavy vertical attacks 138 

repeatedly. This tactic gained dominance after agents discovered that stacking enough damage 139 

enabled a physics engine exploit: slamming opponents through the stage floor, bypassing typical 140

"floor is lava" gene that led to this strategy became scarce over the generations as agents that are not 142 

confined by this restriction were able to combine grounded strategies with "ground slam", achieving 143

3.4 Discussion 145 

These results highlight the unique strengths of EDT-RL: 146 

• Exploration via Mutation: Introducing stochastic reward variation enabled the population 147 

to discover creative, high-impact strategies that were not explicitly encoded in the reward 148 

design. 149 

• Pairwise Crossover: Pairwise crossover encourages diversity while maintaining the ability 150 

to propagate knowledge across the population, allowing efficient use of compute. 151 

• Scalability: Larger populations enabled deeper exploration and more effective recom-152 

bination, supporting rapid behavioral innovation. O(1) communication overhead allows 153 

decentralized and potentially asynchronous training over poorly connected devices. 154 

## 4 Language Model Post-Training - Concept 155 

4.1 Motivation 156 

While pretraining focuses on fitting to large-scale data distributions, post-training aims to steer 157 

pretrained language models toward helpful, safe, and capable behavior—often using only a small 158 

amount of data. At this stage, we are not optimizing for maximum likelihood, but for alignment 159 

with specific goals and constraints. This makes post-training a natural candidate for Evolutionary 160 

Distributed Training (EDT) .161 

Post-training often involves multi-objective tradeoffs (e.g., helpfulness vs. harmlessness), non-162 

differentiable goals (e.g., model judged quality), and the risk of overfitting to narrow supervision 163 

signals. Evolutionary algorithms are well-suited for such settings due to their ability to optimize: 164 

• Multi-objective functions without requiring scalar reward collapse. 165 

• Non-differentiable objectives , such as evaluations from LLM-as-a-judge or task-specific 166 

win-rates. 167 

5Figure 4: EDT post-training framework concept • Policy-space exploration , encouraging behavioral diversity and robustness. 168 

We propose EDT as a flexible wrapper around post-training. Models can be evaluated using any 169 

mix of automated metrics, benchmarks, or preference judgments. Pairwise crossover and mutation 170 

allow exploration across behavioral variants, while decentralized evaluation prevents synchronization 171 

bottlenecks. The goal is not to fine-tune for narrow targets, but to stabilize training, prevent overfitting, 172 

and encourage emergent capabilities through a process of natural evolution. 173 

Due to computational constraints, we are able to conduct experiments at the moment. This direction 174 

could be explored in future work. 175 

## 5 Related Work 176 

5.1 Evolutionary Algorithms and Genetic Optimization 177 

Evolutionary algorithms (EAs) have long been used to solve optimization problems through principles 178 

inspired by natural selection. Early work in this domain includes Genetic Algorithms (GAs) [ 7] which 179 

use crossover and mutation to evolve solutions over generations. In the context of neural networks, 180 

Neuroevolution techniques like NEAT (NeuroEvolution of Augmenting Topologies) [ 20 ] explore 181 

ways to evolve both architectures and weights. More recent large-scale efforts such as OpenAI’s 182 

Evolution Strategies [ 16 ] demonstrated scalability of black-box optimization for deep reinforcement 183 

learning, rivaling traditional policy gradients in some tasks. 184 

While these methods are powerful, they are rarely used in the era of large-scale language models due 185 

to their sample inefficiency and communication overhead. Our proposed Evolutionary Distributed 186 

Training (EDT) differs in that it applies EA concepts specifically to decentralized training settings — 187 

targeting communication-constrained environments and non-differentiable objectives. 188 

5.2 Federated Learning and Distributed LLM Training 189 

Federated learning [ 11 ] enables decentralized optimization by allowing clients to train local models 190 

and periodically synchronize updates with a central server. While originally proposed for privacy-191 

preserving applications, its communication efficiency has inspired variants in large-scale language 192 

model training. 193 

Recent work such as DiLoCo (Distributed Low-Communication) [ 5], shows extremely competitive 194 

performance in Large Language Model training compared to data-parallel, while supported by scaling 195 

laws [3] and open source reproduction [8]. 196 

65.3 Reinforcement Learning and Exploration 197 

Exploration remains a central challenge in reinforcement learning, particularly in environments with 198 

sparse or deceptive rewards. Classical techniques include ϵ-greedy exploration, entropy regularization, 199 

etc. More recently, large-scale self-play has proven effective in discovering complex strategies, as in 200 

AlphaZero [19] and OpenAI Five [13]. 201 

Evolutionary approaches have also been used in RL to promote exploration. Novelty Search [ 9] and 202 

Quality-Diversity algorithms [ 4] evolve agents to encourage behavioral diversity, often outperforming 203 

reward-based methods in hard-exploration domains. Population-Based Training (PBT) [ ? ] introduced 204 

hybrid training-evolution schemes, combining gradient descent with hyperparameter evolution. Our 205 

EDT-RL builds on these ideas, extending the concept to evolve both policy weights and reward 206 

functions in a decentralized fashion. 207 

5.4 Post-Training and Model Steering 208 

Post-training — the phase following large-scale pretraining — is where language models are adapted 209 

to specific behaviors and goals. Typical methods include supervised fine-tuning on curated instruc-210 

tions [ 14 ], Reinforcement Learning from Human Feedback (RLHF) [ ? 14 ], and more recently, 211 

Reinforcement Learning via Verifiable Rewards (RLVR) [18]. 212 

## 6 Limitations 213 

Most of the initial experiments were done on a single laptop RTX4070 by simulating having multiple 214 

workers in a loop. Later, we found a way to scale up to 32 RTX4080s, and was able to complete 215 

most experiments in the RL section. However, when we started experimenting with post-training, 216 

we were noticed by the university and informed that personal research is not allowed on university 217 

infrastructure. Due to limitation in compute, our experimental results are limited. 218 

## 7 Conclusion 219 

We introduced Evolutionary Distributed Training (EDT), a nature-inspired recipe for decentralized 220 

and parallelized model training grounded in the principles of natural evolution — local evaluation, 221 

pairwise reproduction, and mutation. While our initial experiments show that EDT does not offer 222 

significant advantages in language model pertaining, it shows strong promise in reinforcement 223 

learning. In particular, EDT-RL enabled the emergence of novel strategies through reward function 224 

mutation and policy recombination, demonstrating its potential as a tool for structured exploration in 225 

sparse or deceptive environments. 226 

This work represents an early investigation into the possible roles evolutionary algorithms can play 227 

in distributed training systems. We intend to use this paper as a stepping stone towards collecting 228 

feedback and finding potential collaborators. 229
