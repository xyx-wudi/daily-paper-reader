# ABSTRACT 

Open-ended curricula in Reinforcement Learning (RL) aim to train generally-capable agents by identifying tasks that facilitate learning increasingly complex skills. A major challenge when de-signing such curricula is assessing task difficulty relative to the agent’s current learning progress. While previous work has explored using scalar task scores or textual summaries of the agent’s be-havior, here we study a different approach: directly inspecting policy behavior via recorded episode videos. We introduce a simple yet effective instantiation of this approach which leverages a Video Language Model (VLM) to both process these videos and provide curriculum recommendations, which we call Visual Inspection of Policies (VIP). Since videos can naturally contain any number of controllable agents, we empirically study VIP on the StarCraft Multi-Agent Challenge (SMAC). We show that even with a lightweight and openly accessible VLM (VideoLLaMa2-7B), VIP can use policy videos to generate more effective curricula than both its text-only ablation and methods that rely on scalar task scores. # 1 INTRODUCTION 

Many real-world Reinforcement Learning (RL) tasks are difficult or impossible to solve by simply starting from a random policy and directly trying to optimize its downstream performance (Wang et al., 2019; 2020a). These tasks typically require intermediate “stepping stone” skills that are hard to discover with direct optimization and are impossible to identify in advance without extensive domain knowledge. This has motivated research into methods to automatically design open-ended curricula (Wang et al., 2020b; Dennis et al., 2020; Zhang et al., 2024a; Wang et al., 2023) which both discover and solve increasingly complex tasks. The ultimate goal of these methods is to produce generally-capable agents that can quickly adapt to any task in a particular domain, including those unseen in training. 1arXiv:2607.08193v1 [cs.LG] 9 Jul 2026 Preprint. Then, one or more recordings of episodes from the current policy are fed to the VLM, alongside a small textual summary of the agent’s performance (e.g. win rate). The VLM is prompted to recommend the next interesting task, based on its assessment of the current capabilities of the agent. Finally, the raw recommendation from the VLM is compared with the set of tasks available to the curriculum (task space) using a sentence similarity module, to make sure the task is a valid specification. To this end, the curriculum must recommend tasks that are neither too easy nor too hard, so that the agent is presented with the most interesting task that is still solvable. This tradeoff is typically highly dependent on the current learning progress of the agent: tasks that are easy and uninteresting to a very competent agent might still be worth tackling for an agent which has just started learning. To correctly identify tasks that satisfy these properties, previous work has explored both scalar numerical scores (Jiang et al., 2021b; Rutherford et al., 2024; Jiang et al., 2021a) and textual summaries of the agent’s behavior (Zhang et al., 2024a; Aki et al., 2024). The former typically estimate the learning signal that a task could provide, which can be limiting when the reward signal is sparse or deceptive. Many multi-agent games, for example, provide both a sparse reward signal (win rate) and heuristic dense rewards (e.g. eliminating opponents) to aid exploration. However, since these heuristic rewards are not always correlated with winning strategies, relying solely on the learning signal can fail to recognize policies that are close to discovering successful strategies, as shown in Figure 1. Determining learning potential with text-only summaries (Zhang et al., 2024a; Wang et al., 2023) is limited for the same reason. Moreover, these approaches typically build on the human-like priors of LLMs to choose interesting tasks; however, a human in the same position would typically rely on visual cues by observing the agent’s behavior directly. A sports coach, for example, would much rather review the video of a match rather than only read descriptions of an athlete’s performance. Nonetheless, this more direct visual approach has been inaccessible to RL autocurricula until the recent breakthrough of multi-modal LLMs and, more specifically, VLMs. 

Contributions We bridge the gap discussed above by introducing a new approach to open-ended autocurricula: 

Visual Inspection of Policies (VIP), outlined in Figure 2. VIP directly feeds episode videos of the current policy to a Vision Language Model (VLM) to gain direct access to visual information that might be missing in numerical or textual summaries. Additionally, VIP is independent of the number of agents in the environment as long as they are rendered in the same video. We apply VIP to the popular StarCraft Multi-Agent Challenge (SMAC), a complex, strategic, multi-agent collaborative domain with varying number and types of agents, previously unexplored in unsupervised RL autocurricula. We find that VIP outperforms other autocurricula based on learning signal, and that episode videos play a crucial role in its effectiveness. 

# 2 RELATED WORK 

Automatic Curriculum Learning Open-ended autocurricula in Reinforcement Learning have their roots in Auto-matic Curriculum Learning (ACL). Standard ACL methods are supervised: they target a single final task and design a curriculum tailored to that task. Wang et al. (2020b) generates curricula in multi-agent environments by progressively incrementing the number of agents. PORTAL (Wu et al., 2023) is another multi-agent ACL method which constructs 2Preprint. the curriculum by evaluating the similarity between intermediate tasks. STAR-MARL (Li et al., 2026) adopts an LLM to automatically generate appropriate sub-task trees from a known downstream MARL task. 

Open-Ended Autocurricula In contrast to supervised ACL, open-ended autocurricula are fully unsupervised and do not target a single final task, assuming no knowledge on the identity, number or other properties of the downstream tasks. Open-ended autocurricula explicitly aim to continually propose worthwhile tasks to the learning agent and are closely related to the adjacent fields of lifelong learning and Continual Reinforcement Learning (Abel et al., 2023). A popular way to automatically design open-ended curricula is leveraging evolutionary computation methods, such as in POET and enhanced-POET (Wang et al., 2019; 2020a): these co-evolve populations of agents and environments in a divergent process to discover and solve novel problems indefinitely. While highly influential, these works do not provide a clear mechanism for reusing the knowledge acquired by the agent population on unseen tasks, require substantial computational resources, and cannot be easily extended to multi-agent settings. 

Unsupervised Environment Design Unsupervised Environment Design (UED) (Dennis et al., 2020) is a more re-cent framework for open-ended curriculum generation based on minimax regret optimization. UED has been imple-mented both in teacher-student algorithms (Dennis et al., 2020; Jiang et al., 2021a) and in autocurricula based on estimates of learning potential (Jiang et al., 2021b;a; Parker-Holder et al., 2022). Recent work has studied how these estimates might be vulnerable to selecting tasks that are too hard (Rutherford et al., 2024), suggesting an alternative for boolean-outcome deterministic tasks. While these methods are designed for single-agent domains, UED has also been applied to multi-agent settings with MAESTRO (Samvelyan et al., 2023). However, MAESTRO is designed for two-player, adversarial, zero-sum games, and is not applicable to domains with higher or variable number of agents. 

LLM-Guided Autocurricula The breakthrough of LLMs provided a new means to automatically design open-ended curricula, both to evaluate agent progress and propose new tasks. LLM-POET (Aki et al., 2024) combines LLMs with the POET algorithm discussed above. OMNI and OMNI-EPIC (Zhang et al., 2024a; Faldor et al., 2025) employ the LLM as model of interest (MOI) biased towards tasks at the frontier of the agent capabilities that would also be interesting to a human. OMNI-EPIC also experiments with Video LMs but finds them to not be sufficiently reliable. However, their use of Video LMs is relegated to the role of success detectors, again falling into the bucket of compressing rich behaviors into brief summaries. Moreover, OMNI-EPIC relies on the domain exposing a way to program environments in code, which is realistically verified only for simulations that feature such functionality. For example, OMNI-EPIC cannot be applied to any simulation benchmark where physics and/or world dynamics are not properly decoupled from environment variants. Dharna et al. (2025) propose a method that combines the power of the human priors contained in Foundation Models (FMs) with self-play. This method, however, scales with the number of agents and is not trivially extensible to variable or high number of agents. Zhang et al. (2024b) propose a method with some similarities to VIP, which uses a VLM to guide open-ended RL training experiments, including recommending intermediate tasks in a curriculum. However, this method relies on manually identifying a set of skills before the start of the learning process, requiring significant prior domain knowledge and manual design. 

Positioning All of the approaches discussed above use aggregate information on the current learning progress to choose the next best task. Scalar task scoring such as in UED might fail to capture nuanced information that is not directly linked to learning signals — on the other hand, LLM-based methods mostly focus on describing the candidate tasks, rather than describing the learning progress of the current policy. In this work, we instead take advantage of VLMs to directly gauge the learning progress of the agents by analyzing episode videos of the current policy. 

# 3 BACKGROUND 

Open-ended Reinforcement Learning Reinforcement Learning (RL) (Richard S. Sutton, 2018) deals with learn-ing solutions to sequential decision problems, usually modeled via Markov Decision Processes (MDPs) or Partially-Observable Markov Decision Processes (POMDPs). While (PO)MDPs can model a wide range of tasks, they assume that all components are stationary throughout the interaction with the agent. This fails to model many realistic sce-narios which feature variations of one or more components after training, requiring the agent to generalize to unseen situations (Franc ¸ois-Lavet et al., 2018). These settings can instead be modeled with a distribution ∆(Θ) over a set Θ

of (PO)MDPs. Recent work has then started to consider cases where only the set Θ of possible (PO)MDPs is known at training time, while the target distribution ∆(Θ) is either unknown or absent, leading to open-ended RL (Dennis et al., 2020; Jiang, 2023; Cobbe et al., 2020; Baker et al., 2020; Hafner et al., 2024; Bauer et al., 2023). In open-ended RL, agents do not have a predefined final objective and must adapt promptly to any possible situation in the given domain. Large Language Models Large Language Models (LLMs) have emerged as a significant advancement in the field of Natural Language Processing (Minaee et al., 2024; Naveed et al., 2025). LLMs are transformers (Vaswani et al., 2017) with a large number of learning parameters. Although LLMs can be discriminative or generative models, it is common to refer to the latter as simply LLMs, which we also do here. Large Language Models demonstrated consistently superior performances across several tasks when compared to previous techniques (Brown et al., 2020). On the other hand, LLMs require significantly more computational power (Samsi et al., 2023; Hu et al., 2024) due to the transformer architecture (Ansar et al., 2024; Schiavella et al., 2024). This can pose severe practical limitations to their use both in industry and research. Although LLMs were initially developed to only process and output text, the recent breakthrough of Multi-modal LLMs (Caffagni et al., 2024) has allowed them to process multi-modal data, such as images and videos. In this work we specifically use Video Language Models (VLMs): multi-modal LLMs that accept text and videos as input modalities and output text. 

# 4 VISUAL INSPECTION OF POLICIES 

Here we present Visual Inspection of Policies (VIP), a new method to generate curricula using a Vision Language Model to inspect one or more episode videos of the current policies. VIP employs a single VLM to both analyze episode videos and recommend appropriate interesting tasks for the curriculum based on its human-like priors. As depicted intuitively in Figure 1, the added visual information allows VIP to take into account learning progress cues that might be missing from other behavior summarization modalities. VIP’s intentional simplicity makes it easy to test this intuition empirically with a text-only ablation while still generating effective curricula (Section 5). An overview of VIP is presented in Algorithm 1. First, policies are trained on the most recently recommended task with a Reinforcement Learning algorithm (Train-RL). The resulting policies are then evaluated on the current task to gather one or more videos and other information. This multi-modal information is then fed to the VLM, which is prompted to select the task that best facilitates learning (VLM-Next-Task). To mitigate hallucinations and formatting errors, the raw task recommendation of the VLM is then passed through a sentence-similarity module (Sanitize-Task) to obtain a valid task specification. The initial task in the curriculum can be either directly queried from the VLM or manually 4Preprint. set where sensible, while policies are initialized according to the low-level RL algorithm (Train-RL), typically by randomly sampling model parameter values. In principle, VIP is agnostic to the number of agents in the environment, as there is no hard constraint on the number of agents that may appear in a single video. In a multiplayer team game, for example, the VLM can directly process the video performances of the whole team, spending the same compute required to analyze a single policy. More generally, VIP requires prompting the VLM for N task recommendations, where N is the number of distinct models being trained — any environment with a variable number of agents sharing a single model would then result in N = 1 . Another benefit of VIP’s simplicity is that it introduces no new hyper-parameters in addition to those needed by the VLM and low-level RL algorithm. This is especially desirable for open-ended autocurricula since one of its overarching goals is precisely replacing brute-force grid search and similar supervised ways to acquire domain knowledge. We discuss this more in depth at the start of Section 5 and in Section 5.4. VIP also makes the basic assumption that the environment provides at least partial image observations or any kind of rendering functionality, which is trivially verified in most simulated and real-world RL domains. Finally, the effectiveness of VIP largely relies on the VLM, as with any LLM-based autocurricula (Zhang et al., 2024a). By design, such methods cannot be truly robust to the choice of LLM/VLM, as they must naturally inherit specific weaknesses of the model to gain access to its strengths. At the same time, the LLM should be chosen such that it does not become a bottleneck in the curriculum. For this reason, our experiments employ a lightweight, openly accessible VLM which results in task recommendations only taking ∼1% of the total curriculum training time (Appendix E). 4.1 PROMPTING AND SANITIZATION 

In order to produce sensible task suggestions, the VLM is provided with a system prompt before the start of the curriculum. This system prompt should contain minimal information about the domain, the set of possible tasks, the syntax and semantics of the chosen task representation, and the goal of the VLM. The latter, in particular, can lead to curricula with different trade-offs between choosing novel tasks and tasks that are easy to learn for the current agents. At each step of the curriculum, the VLM is then fed a task prompt , which should contain one or more video recordings of the current policies. Other numerical or textual information can also be included in the prompt, such as win rate or other performance metrics, estimates of learning potential or novelty, etc. In this work we only add the win rate and a list of the previously selected tasks to the videos. The latter turned out to be necessary in our experiments due to the context capabilities of the chosen VLM (see Appendix F.3). Even if provided with a precise description of the desired task specification format, there is no guarantee that the VLM will follow it in all outputs. Moreover, the VLM might hallucinate task specifications, especially when the syntax allows to express more tasks than those actually available. For example, the domain we chose for our experiments uses task specifications with letters and numbers, but not all combinations correspond to valid configurations. Abasic solution, which we adopt in this work, is to use a simple module that outputs the best-matching valid task description from a VLM answer based on sentence similarity. This module computes the similarity between the sinusoidal embeddings (Vaswani et al., 2017) of the VLM output and the candidate task names, returning the candidate with the highest similarity score. In our experiments, we also set a threshold such that when no task has sufficient similarity to the available ones, a random one is chosen, although this fallback mechanism was never triggered. Since the similarity module was able to recover all of the VLM failure modes in our experiments (Appendix F.2), we have refrained from employing alternatives such as iterative prompting (Wang et al., 2023), as these would have introduced unnecessary risks and additional VLM queries. The similarity module may also fail to recover hallucinations when the task specifications are heavily out-of-distribution w.r.t. the embedding model. In practice, this is rarely the case since most task specifications resemble natural language or human-readable configurations. Iterative prompting or training a suitable embedding model are both valid alternatives for edge cases with more obscure task specification formats. This simple solution also does not immediately apply to non-enumerable task spaces — these can also easily be handled with iterative prompting. Moreover, many non-enumerable task spaces adopt task specifications such that all syntactically correct specifications are valid task and thus require no sanitization. 

# 5 EXPERIMENTS 

We investigate the following research questions: • Q1 : Does adding visual behavioral information (policy videos) to textual summaries result in more effective curricula? • Q2 : Can VIP outperform unsupervised autocurricula methods based on scalar task scores? 5Preprint. We study these questions on the StarCraft Multi-Agent Challenge (SMAC) (Samvelyan et al., 2019). SMAC is a cooperative, strategic multi-player game that tests individual and collaborative agent capabilities at several time scales, making it a popular benchmark for Multi-Agent Reinforcement Learning. The high variety in agent composition and environment configuration, however, makes SMAC particularly challenging for transfer and/or curriculum learning — to the best of our knowledge, no previous work has tested unsupervised autocurricula on this benchmark. We answer Q1 and Q2 by comparing VIP against: (i) its text-only ablation; (ii) two popular variants of Robust Prioritized Level Replay (Jiang et al., 2021a); (iii) control baselines such as random curricula and training from scratch. All comparisons are performed both on held-out tasks (Sections 5.2) and on tasks commonly seen by many curricula (Section 5.3). In all curricula, agents are trained for 10 7 environment steps on each task using Multi-Agent Proximal Policy Opti-mization (MAPPO) (Yu et al., 2022), a popular MARL algorithm. Since zero-shot transfer between configurations is unlikely to succeed in SMAC (Wu et al., 2023), we instead test generalization of policies produced by each curricu-lum by evaluating their win rate after fine-tuning on each test task (Baker et al., 2020; Wang et al., 2019) for another 

10 7 steps. While this is a well-established protocol for SMAC (Yu et al., 2022; Wu et al., 2023), it introduces the issue of tuning hyper-parameters when applied to unsupervised curricula. In fact, by definition, these cannot assume knowledge of the downstream task and thus cannot collect environment interactions for the purpose of grid search or similar methods. Moreover, optimal hyper-parameter values in SMAC are not reusable for multiple tasks as they often differ widely (Yu et al., 2022). Our main experiments in Sections 5.2 and 5.3 share the same default MAPPO hyper-parameters among all considered curricula (Table 5), with the objective of avoiding reliance on downstream task knowledge. We also conduct a secondary set of experiments that compare VIP with supervised grid search and discuss the results in Section 5.4. All fine-tuning curves and final performances are reported with 95% confidence intervals over 5 independent seeds. 5.1 SETUP 

Environment The environment of SMAC consists of two opposing teams of troops, “allied” against “enemies”, each shooting at the opposing faction within a 2D continuous plane, with the objective of eliminating all opponents. Learning agents correspond to the allied troops, while enemy troops are controlled by the game AI. Troops may vary in type and number across both teams. More details on SMAC as a set of (PO)MDPs, such as observation and action spaces, are contained in Appendix A.3. For the purposes of computing the win rate, a round is won when all enemy troops are dead; all other cases are considered losses, including draws. To ensure fairness with respect to prior domain knowledge assumptions, we record episode videos with the built-in rendering scheme of SMAC, although it can sometimes be limited in accurately depicting the state of the game (see Appendix A.3). 

Task Space Tasks in SMAC are identified by two configuration parameters: the map , which controls allied and enemy troop compositions, and the difficulty , which controls the behavior of the game AI. Troops of different types have substantially different capabilities and thus require completely different strategies to be piloted successfully. We follow the map naming convention of Vinyals et al. (2017) — for example, the map name 3s5z vs 3s6z specifies allies having 3 troops of type s (Stalkers) and 5 troops of type z (Zealot), and enemies having 3 Stalkers and 6 zealots. Maps where teams have the same composition only report the first portion, e.g. 3s5z is equivalent to 3s5z vs 3s5z .The difficulty is a simple integer in the range 1-10. Counterintuitively, there are some instances of maps being harder to solve at specific lower difficulties (see Appendix B for examples). This is likely due to the game AI levels being tailored to human players. Note that even though infinite allied and enemy compositions exist, not all of them are valid tasks in SMAC. This means that the actual task space is a finite and enumerable subset of the full space of all conceivable compositions. Note that an enumerable task space embedded in an infinite task space is not necessarily easier for curriculum design, as it might miss better tasks that are outside the subset. At the same time, the full task space might contain more distractors, which in turn means that neither configuration can be definitively said to be easier or harder. To properly evaluate generalization, we split the available maps into an 80% available for training (12 maps) and a 20% for testing fine-tuning on unseen maps (3 maps). The subdivision is reported in Table 4 and further motivated in Section 5.2. 

VLM We use VideoLLaMa2-7B, the 7-billion quantized version of the openly accessible VideoLLaMa2 (Cheng et al., 2024), as the VLM for VIP, paired with the sentence similarity model from Transformers (2021). Since VideoLLaMa2 does not support video batching, each prompt is paired with one video of an episode sampled from the current team policy on the last selected task in the curriculum. Videos are fed as-is to the VLM without any pre-processing. Despite these limitations, our experiments show that this VLM is sufficient for VIP to outperform the considered baselines. This choice also ensures that the VLM is not a bottleneck in the curriculum: in all our runs, MAPPO training strongly dominated raw compute times by taking up around 99% of the time spent for each VIP run, whereas VLM inference was limited to the remaining 1% (see Appendix E). Baselines As mentioned above, we answer Q1 by comparing VIP against a text-only ablation. The only difference between VIP and this ablation is that the video input and phrases mentioning it are omitted from the prompts, allowing to measure the contribution of episode videos to VIP’s effectiveness. We also compare VIP against Robust Prioritized Level Replay (PLR ⊥) (Jiang et al., 2021a), an Unsupervised Environment Design method (Dennis et al., 2020) that selects tasks according to a scalar score function evaluated from policy rollouts. Here we compare to PLR ⊥ with two common choices for the score function: Positive Value Loss (PVL) and Maximum Monte Carlo (MaxMC) (Rutherford et al., 2024). For the sake of simplicity, in the following we refer to Robust PLR as simply PLR. 5.2 FINE -TUNING ON UNSEEN TASKS 

We study generalization to out-of-distribution tasks using the held-out maps: 10m vs 11m , 3s vs 4z , and 3s5z ,all at difficulty 5. These maps feature a good variety of team scale and troop types, with two of them also carrying a numbers disadvantage, and are considered among the most challenging in literature (Yu et al., 2022; Sun et al., 2021; Kuba et al., 2021). We set the game AI difficulty to 5 since, counterintuitively, it is more challenging than difficulty 7 for MARL (see Appendix B for details). Note that these maps did not appear at any difficulty in any curricula. Fine-tuning curves are reported in Figure 3, while evaluation win rates after fine-tuning are reported in Table 1. On maps 3s vs 4z and 3s5z , agents fine-tuned from VIP curricula learn high-performing solutions ( ∼80% win rate), while almost all baselines exhibit near-zero win rates. The text-only ablation performs substantially worse than its multi-modal counterpart on these two maps, demonstrating that videos are crucial to the success of VIP and confirming the intuition illustrated in Figure 1. On the remaining map 10m vs 11m , VIP still obtains the highest performance, comparably with its text-only ablation and random curricula. To better contextualize this result, we note that: (i) 10m vs 11m is the only unseen map with marine troops, often considered among the hardest (Yu et al., 2022); (ii) marine troops appear in ∼70% of tasks from the random curricula and ∼15% of tasks from the text-only ablation curricula, while only appearing once in VIP curricula as the initial task. In all maps, both PLR variants obtain consistently poor performances, showing how scalar scoring can lack essential information when selecting tasks for a curriculum. These complement the above results by mea-suring to what extent each trained policy was able to retain the knowledge used to solve tasks in the early or middle stages of the curriculum. To this end, we selected the following configurations: 3m at difficulty 6, 3s vs 3z at dif-ficulty 7 and 3s vs 5z at difficulty 7. 3m is consistently seen in the early stages of all curricula. Maps 3s vs 3z 

and 3s vs 5z feature a mismatch in troop types between allies and enemies (paired with a numbers disadvantage in the latter) which significantly increases their complexity (Yu et al., 2022). The difficulty was chosen as the average among the difficulties chosen by all curricula on each map. We note that the second and third maps were never seen by the PLR-PVL baseline. We still selected these for evaluation since PLR-PVL curricula had no intersections with other curricula outside of 3m at difficulty 6 (see Appendix G for more details). Only strictly selecting tasks seen by all methods, including PLR-PVL, would have thus not yielded a meaningful comparison due to 3m not being sufficiently challenging. Even though these same configurations were seen at different stages for different curricula, all curricula included them at the same or later stages than VIP, ensuring no trivial advantage for the latter. Fine-tuning curves are reported in Figures 4, while evaluation win rates after fine-tuning are reported in Table 2. In all maps, VIP still consistently achieves the highest performance. On map 3s vs 5z , our method fine-tunes to around 35% win rate in the allocated number of steps, while all others fail to find any meaningful solution. On map 3s vs 3z , VIP shows a significantly better final win rate along with a more stable and faster convergence. All considered methods achieve high win rates on 3m . This is expected, since 3m is widely recognized as being among the simplest maps in SMAC. Nonetheless, PLR agents show a high starting point in the fine-tuning curve but significantly worse final performances with respect to all other methods. # 6 CONCLUSIONS 

We introduced Visual Inspection of Policies (VIP), a new method to automatically generate open-ended curricula for Reinforcement Learning (RL) agents. VIP uses a VLM to directly inspect episode videos from the current policy and recommend tasks tailored to the current learning progress. It is also independent of the number of controllable agents in the environment as long as they are rendered in a single video. We have studied the effectiveness of VIP empirically with a lightweight and openly accessible VLM on a complex, strategic multi-agent domain, showing that policy videos play a key role in VIP’s performance through its text-only ablation. At the same time, our results show that VIP outperforms autocurricula which rely on scalar task scores. Future work will investigate the use of other modalities and multi-modal LLMs for unsupervised autocurricula, possi-bly combining more than one LLM such as in more complex text-only approaches (Zhang et al., 2024a; Faldor et al., 2025). These developments are likely to require more advanced sanitization techniques, especially when considering larger or infinite task spaces. More in general, we envision VIP as only the tip of the iceberg in leveraging VLMs and other multi-modal LLMs as a natural avenue for open-ended Reinforcement Learning: much like text is only one of the modalities through which humans guide their unsupervised learning processes, rich visual and multi-modal information is also essential when evaluating the progress of any artificial learning agent.

SMAC is a partially observable environment, with each agent retrieving local observations from their own field of view. Local observations contain, for each ally and enemy troop within sight: Euclidean distance , distance along x ,

distance along y , health , shield , unit typology . The full state of the environment includes the above information for all entities in the map, without any sight restrictions. The action space is discrete, with available actions being: move north , move east , move west , move south , attack i (one for each enemy i), stop , no-op .The built-in rendering scheme for SMAC is minimal, which has both positive and negative effects. On the one hand, it does not employ purely cosmetic representations, which makes it free of distractors. At the same time, it might also lose information in representing the state of the game (see Figure 5). Since the size and semantics of the observations and actions depend on the number and types of troops, transferring policies between maps is not possible in vanilla SMAC. To this end, we have padded the observations and action spaces of all maps to a unique size, corresponding to the map with the highest number of agents ( 27m vs 30m ). The padding simply consisted of zeros which have no effects on environment state transitions. Even though this is the simplest solution to the size mismatch between maps, it still adds distractors to each task, which can lower the sample efficiency of MARL training. 

# B DIFFICULTY 5 IS MORE CHALLENGING THAN 7

Existing literature usually sets the difficulty of most SMAC maps to 7 (Yu et al., 2022; Wu et al., 2023). Here we motivate our choice of instead choosing difficulty 5 for our main experiments on unseen maps (Section 5.2): while experimenting with the game AI difficulty setting, we found that difficulty 5 poses a harder challenge to MARL agents than difficulty 7. To confirm this, we trained MAPPO from scratch on both difficulties on the maps used as unseen maps in our main experiments. Results are shown in Figure 6 and confirm that difficulty 5 is significantly harder on these maps for MARL agents. While this may seem counterintuitive at first, we note that the SMAC game AI difficulty levels were originally developed for human players rather than RL agents. This might make it possible that some strategies of the game AI at difficulty 5 are easier to beat for human players, but may be much harder for MARL. Vice-versa, the game AI might play out better against humans rather than MARL agents on difficulty 7.

F.3 PROMPT TEMPLATES 

We follow the standard practice of providing the VLM with an initial system prompt , followed by multiple prompts, each asking for a task recommendation for the curriculum. The system prompt assigns the VLM the role of a “video game expert”, whose duty consists of evaluating the performances of RL agents by means of inspecting policy videos and other information. The system prompt also includes information on the domain such as a small description of the SMAC environment, the list of difficulties, train maps, etc. The system prompt also specifies the desired format for the task recommendation output. At each curriculum step, the task prompt reiterates a summary of the task assigned to the VLM and asks for a task recommendation based on the provided visual and other information. Prompt templates for the system prompt and task prompts are reported below. The inclusion of repeated contextual information, such as the list of previously selected tasks, was needed in our experiments due to limited context capabilities of the 7B model. We confirmed that this was the case by simulating interactions with different levels of context repetition in task prompts. Specifically, we compared: (i) no context information; (ii) previous step context (last selected task); (iii) complete context information (full list of previous tasks). We report that the cases with limited (ii) or no context information (i) showed a lack of awareness of past interactions by repeatedly selecting tasks that were selected in the previous few steps. Instead, when provided with complete contextual information (iii), the 7B model properly accounted for it as expected, motivating the final task prompt template shown below. Each textual prompt during the curricula was paired with a single video 1024x1024 at 25 frames per second; while video length varied based on the episode length, in our experiments all videos were 1-10 seconds long, since the maximum episode timeout for a SMAC map is 250 steps. F.3.1 SYSTEM PROMPT TEMPLATE 

You are a video game expert in charge of evaluating the performances of a reinforcement learning algorithm through the visualization of recorded videos of the algorithm’s actions within a StarcraftII environment. You must evaluate its performance on that specific setting, i.e. on that specific map, and its potential ability to generalize to other maps of the same environment, i.e. game. The environment is a simplified version of the StarcraftII game: two adversarial teams fight, the first team losing all its components loses the battle. StarcraftII is a strategic, military-inspired, game where attacking takes the form of shooting or close combat, depending on the typology of troops de-ployed. Its reinforcement learning environment is multi-agent, specifically the algorithm commands all the agents of a single team, thereafter restricting the setting to exclusively a cooperative one. The videos of this environment that will be shown to you depict circles of two colours: on one side the green circles compose the allies team, thereafter the one commanded by the reinforcement learning agents, on the other side the red circles compose the enemies team. Here we provide additional details on the poor performances of PLR curricula observed in the main experiments. To showcase differences with VIP-generated curricula, below we report one example curriculum for each PLR baseline and one example curriculum from VIP, with each line containing the chosen task as the pair (map, difficulty). We also report the task distributions of these examples in Table 6. While the distributions highlight the low variety of curricula from PLR (especially with the PVL score), the examples theselves show the PLR curricula to be much more erratic in the choice of both maps and difficulty: several repeated tasks are often not adjacent in the curriculum. On the other hand, VIP generates a more linear progression both in maps and difficulty, often repeating the same map with increasing difficulty and almost never going back to a task in non-adjacent curriculum steps. We hypothesize the poorly-generated curricula from PLR are due to a mixture of: (i) lack of information present in scalar score; and (ii) scalar score themselves being known to be vulnerable to task that are too hard (Rutherford et al., 2024).
